GlucoMood

Health tracking reimagined, connecting your glucose and your emotions.

GLUCOMOOD TIMELINE

1

The Problem with Dexcom

GlucoMood started from my own experience wearing a Dexcom and living with the reality of checking glucose data regularly. I kept thinking that something so tied to how you feel physically and emotionally should not have to look so plain, clinical, and disconnected from real life.

2

Humanizing the Data

A lot of health apps feel like they only care about showing numbers, but not the person behind those numbers. As someone with ADHD, I knew I wanted something that felt more visually engaging, more alive, and easier to emotionally connect with, because design really affects whether something feels supportive or draining.

3

The Mood Connection

That is where the idea for GlucoMood came from: a glucose-tracking app that feels more vibrant, mood-aware, and human. I wanted to build something that still respected the seriousness of health data, but did it in a way that felt more personal and expressive instead of cold and sterile.

4

A New Visual Language

From the beginning, my goal was not just to make a prettier Dexcom-style app. I wanted to create a space where glucose data, mood, energy, and day-to-day patterns could feel more connected, so the experience made more sense as part of someone’s actual life.

5

Steps

I built this feature around a simple question I kept asking myself: what if people could feel their steps in terms of food, not just numbers. I designed a food form where someone could choose, say, a chocolate chip cookie, and the app would translate their daily steps into how much of that cookie their movement “covers.” I used common guidance on steps, calories, and walking to estimate rough equivalents, then rounded and simplified everything so it felt human and easy to grasp, not like a math lecture. As I tried this with people, I watched their reactions closely and shifted the tone away from guilt toward curiosity and encouragement, focusing on phrases like “your walk today covered most of this cookie” instead of “you didn’t walk enough.” For me, this project was about turning abstract health data into a friendly, relatable story that helps people understand their bodies without feeling judged.

6

Emotive States

This project feels personal because it comes from something I actually wear on my body and think about in real life, not just a random app idea. I wanted to design from that place of lived experience, where the product feels like it understands the emotional side of tracking health too.

7

Mimi: The AI Companion

One of the biggest future parts of GlucoMood is the AI assistant. My vision is for it to help users during high or low sugar moments, especially after hours when someone might be overwhelmed, scared, or unsure what to do next, while being very clear that it is there for support and guidance, not to replace a doctor or medical care.

8

Metabolic Intelligence

I want the AI assistant to feel calm, practical, and compassionate. Instead of acting like it knows everything, it would help users think through what may be happening, offer commonly helpful next steps, and encourage them to seek medical attention when needed.

9

The Bigger Vision

Long term, I want GlucoMood to become more than just a phone app. One of the future features I care about most is bringing it to Apple Watch, because glucose tracking often happens in quick moments, and I think having that kind of support right on the wrist could make the experience feel even more immediate and useful.

What I’m trying to accomplish

At its core, GlucoMood is my attempt to make health tracking feel less cold and intimidating, and more human, supportive, and emotionally aware. I want it to be something that helps people understand their glucose in a way that feels more personal, especially for users like me who need an experience that is not only functional, but actually feels good to use.
